Airway Pressure Release Ventilation (APRV)

  • Summary
  • Settings
  • Spontaneous Breathing
APRV is a spontaneous breathing mode that uses high mean airway pressure and periodic timed pressure releases to enhance alveolar recruitment), redistribute lung volumes and improve gas exchange in patients with pediatric acute respiratory distress syndrome and refractory hypoxemia. APRV is only effective when the patient is spontaneously breathing.
  1. Unlike conventional, all APRV settings may impact ventilation and oxygenation
  2. P HIGH : Pressure high allows “super CPAP” that aids in alveolar recruitment during spontaneous breathing
  3. P LOW : Pressure low is the release pressure that allows the ventilator valve to release pressure and provide exhalation of gases (Tidal volume = ∆P HIGH - P LOW )
  4. T HIGH : Time high determines the duration that the patient can breath at P HIGH and the number of pressure releases per minute (respiratory rate)
  5. T LOW : Determines the duration that the patient breaths at P LOW

Spontaneous breathing efforts result in the generation of negative intra thoracic pressures which may have the following benefits:

  • Re-expansion of dependent “diseased” lung segments
  • Improve pulmonary blood flow and decreased V/Q mismatch
  • Improve cardiac output

Efforts should be made to augment spontaneous breathing efforts in order to be successful with this mode (ie removed paralytics, reduced sedation, provide Diamox)

Initial Settings
  • FiO2 = as required, usually ≥ 60 % with ALI
  • P HIGH = same as P PLATEAU or PIP (if unable to obtain Pplat) ; P HIGH may be set lower in cases of extreme air-leak; higher P HIGH (~30 cmH2O) may be required in sicker patients to allow recruitment and maintain adequate exhaled tidal volumes (5-6 mL/kg). Patients that are obese or have severe chest wall restriction may require ~30 cmH2O for chest wall expansion
  • P LOW = Always set at 0 cmH2O; pressure seen in the lung will always be higher than zero if T LOW is set appropriately (see below); applying pressure above 0 cmH2O may affect valve performance and increase expiratory resistance
  • T HIGH = 4 to 6 seconds initially; adjusts number of releases per minute or “machine rate”; patients with elevated CO2 may need shorter T HIGH and patients with poor oxygenation may need longer T HIGH
  • T LOW = long enough to get returned VT, but NOT long enough to de-recruit. Typically, 0.2- 0.5 seconds in children (see below)

Do not set PSV or automatic tubing compensation; pressure above T HIGH may obviate the beneficial effects of pleural pressure

Check for expiratory flow termination using T LOW
  • Make sure that the end-expiratory flow waveform is “clipped” at 75% of peak expiratory flow (short time constants; ie ARDS) and 50% of the peak expiratory flow (long time constant; ie bronchiolitis) so that back pressure is applied to the lung compartments
  • Patients with severe refractory hypoxemia and P HIGH (30-35) may benefit from higher clipping ~70% of peak expiratory flow
  • Freeze screen and scroll to different points to observe numeric value of expiratory flow on the Draeger
  • Observe the expiratory flow waveform so it does not return to zero flow before the P HIGH is re- established
  • Recheck every two hours or after major changes in compliance, resistance (suctioning), and settings changes

Optimize oxygenation following initiation

  • Wean P HIGH once the lung is “open”
  • Observe exhaled tidal volume during the “release” phase of the breath to determine improvement or decrease in compliance and resistance
